# Pawlish — Preset Plan

Independent neighborhood dog grooming salon. Appointment-based, small two-to-four-groomer team, repeat clientele on 4–8 week cycles. The name "Pawlish" leans into grooming-as-styling — a wink toward "polish" without going precious — and signals competence and care, not cartoon cuteness or fluffy-pink spa.

## Phase 0 — Industry Strategy Brief

- **Business archetype:** Booking-based local service with a recurring-appointment cadence. Small storefront, two to four groomers, calendar-bound capacity (each groomer sees 4–6 dogs/day). Customers don't churn through — they pick a salon and stay for years, returning every 4–8 weeks. New-client intake is the bottleneck, not retention.

- **Primary conversion:** Book a grooming appointment (online request or phone). Secondary: a quick-question inquiry ("do you handle a 70lb golden doodle?", "do you take cats?") that converts later. The site's job is to clear those vetting questions and produce a booking request — not to close on the first visit.

- **Trust mechanism:** Handling skill above all. A pet-parent is leaving a family member with strangers for two-to-four hours. Trust is built by: (1) visible groomer experience and credentials, (2) explicit handling philosophy ("low-stress, never sedated, breaks if needed"), (3) before/after evidence of breed-specific cut quality, (4) hygiene and facility cleanliness, (5) longevity ("grooming this neighborhood since 2014"). Reviews matter but they're confirmation, not the lead.

- **Decision mode:** Considered and emotional. Pet owners shop carefully for the first appointment (asking friends, reading reviews, sometimes visiting first), then become loyal for years. First-visit anxiety is the dominant emotion the site has to defuse. Pricing transparency matters because grooming costs vary widely by breed/coat and unclear pricing reads as "they'll surprise me at pickup."

- **Brand personality:** Warm, calm, quietly competent. The feel of a grooming salon where the dogs come back wagging — staff who know every dog by name, a clean shop with good light, scissors in a leather roll, a chalkboard with the day's appointments. NOT a pink-bow boutique, NOT a clinical vet's office, NOT a corporate franchise (PetSmart-like), NOT a children's-toy palette. Slightly stylish — the name "Pawlish" earns a confident, slightly editorial brand voice rather than a cutesy one.

- **Content posture:** Image-led for the proof surface (before/after, dogs in the salon, groomers at work) — but balanced with text-led trust content (handling philosophy, groomer bios, what-to-expect FAQs). The site is half "look at the cuts" and half "here's how we treat your dog." Pure-gallery would feel shallow; pure-text would waste the strongest asset (cute dogs that look great after a session).

- **Audience model:** Three overlapping audiences the site must serve without picking favorites: (1) **purebred owners** with specific cut requirements (poodle, doodle, schnauzer, terrier — they care about whether the groomer knows the breed standard); (2) **mixed-breed owners** who want a clean, comfortable dog and don't care about a "show cut" but care a lot about gentle handling; (3) **senior or anxious-dog owners** who specifically need a low-stress provider and may have been turned away elsewhere. Cats are intentionally **out of scope** for this preset — many grooming salons are dogs-only and being clear about that is a feature, not a limitation.

- **Required page jobs:**
  - **Home** — establish voice, show the proof (cuts), say what services exist, surface the booking path, hint at the team
  - **Services** — what we actually do (full groom, bath-and-tidy, nail-and-ear, de-shed, hand-stripping, puppy first-groom). Each service explained in a sentence or two.
  - **Pricing** — by size/coat tier (toy/small/medium/large/giant — or short/medium/long coat). Industry expects ranges, not single prices. Honest about why prices vary.
  - **Groomers** — who'll be handling the dog. Per-groomer: years grooming, certifications, breeds/coats they specialize in, a one-line philosophy. This is the trust mechanism made human.
  - **Gallery / Before & After** — the proof page. Dogs from the salon, ideally arranged so cut quality is obvious. This is where image-led posture pays off.
  - **First Visit / FAQ** — vaccinations required, what to bring, drop-off vs. wait, how long it takes, what happens if my dog is anxious, late-fee policy. First-visit anxiety lives here.
  - **Contact / Book** — booking request form + phone + map + hours.

  **Seven pages**, but Pricing and Services may combine into a single page if the test holds (see Phase 1). FAQ may live as its own page rather than embedded — it's load-bearing for this industry in a way it isn't for, say, a restaurant.

- **No-go patterns:**
  - Pink-and-bow "doggy spa" aesthetic — fluffy fonts, rhinestone vibes, baby pink + lavender
  - Cartoon paw prints, comic-sans-adjacent typography, mascot illustrations
  - Children's-brand primary-color palette (red/yellow/blue chunkiness)
  - Sterile vet-clinic visual language — pure white + medical blue with stethoscope-style imagery
  - Stock-photo golden retrievers in a field; stock-photo "happy customers"
  - Generic spa-website palette (warm beige + script fonts) — wrong industry register
  - Hard-sell "BOOK NOW" stacks — this is a relationship business, not a daily-deal site
  - Cartoon scissors, cartoon shampoo bottles, illustrated pet icons used decoratively
  - Any visual that suggests sedation, restraints, or rough handling — even unintentionally

- **Opener candidates:**
  - **Proof-first opener** — lead with a single great before/after or a 3-up grid of cuts the salon is known for. The dogs are the headline. Strong fit for image-led posture.
  - **Editorial-text opener** — short statement of philosophy ("Calm grooming for dogs who'd rather be home.") with a small proof element beside it. Quieter; works against the cartoony defaults of the industry.
  - **Team-led opener** — the groomers as the welcome ("Mara, Dani, and Rae have been grooming this neighborhood since 2014"). Fits trust-by-people but risks repeating the Noteworthy preset's pattern.
  - **Booking-first opener** — date/availability strip up top. Fits the appointment model but reads transactional and fights the "calm, considered" personality. Probably wrong here.

  Leaning toward a **proof-first composition**: a strong cuts-led visual paired with a one-sentence handling statement and a clear "Book a groom" CTA. Image-led is the right posture, but the imagery must be **the dogs from the salon**, not stock.

- **Closing pattern:** Calm invitation rather than urgent CTA. Booking is the action, but the language should match the brand voice — something like "Bring your dog in." or "Tell us about your dog." Reinforced by a low-friction contact surface (phone, hours, the form) rather than a hero-sized booking widget. For pages where a CTA isn't right, a soft "next step" close (e.g. "Read about our groomers" → "See our work") works better than forcing a booking nudge on every page.

## Phase 1 — Plan

### Identity

- **Name:** Pawlish
- **Tagline-seed:** "Calm grooming for dogs who'd rather be home."
- **Brand world:** A confident, slightly stylish neighborhood dog grooming salon. Hardwood floors, eucalyptus on the counter, copper grooming shears in a leather roll, dogs who walk in apprehensive and walk out lighter. Warm earth tones, not pink-spa. Competent, not cute.

### Industry Translation

- **Opener role:** Proof-first and literal. The page leads with **`comparison-slider`** as the opener — a single fullwidth interactive before/after of a dog the salon recently groomed. Visitors immediately drag the handle and *see* the work; trust is built without a single sentence of copy. No banner, no split-hero, no stock photography. The opener is the proof.
- **Home priority order:** proof opener → the services in plain language → the longevity numbers → a browsable cuts strip → parent voices → soft close.
- **Pricing posture:** Transparent and structured by **dog size** (the way every grooming salon actually prices). `content-switcher` toggles three size brackets, each with the same service lineup so customers can find their dog in one click. No "call for a quote" fog.
- **Trust posture:** Two-pronged. (1) Visual: before/after evidence, dogs from the salon, the room itself. (2) Human: deep groomer bios with credentials, years, breed specializations, and one explicit handling-philosophy section so anxious-dog parents know they're in the right place.
- **Tone of copy:** Direct, calm, dog-aware. No "fur babies," no "pawesome," no exclamation points in headings. Industry-fluent but not insider-jargon.

### Widget Discipline

After surveying the 52-widget catalog and tallying usage across the existing 30 presets, this plan deliberately:

**Bans (strict):**
- **No `banner`** anywhere. The most overused widget in the catalog (112 instances). Pawlish proves an Arch site can open and close without it.
- **No `split-hero`**. Wrong opener register for a proof-led, image-led brand — and nine other presets already use it.
- **No `image-text`** (67 catalog uses) and **no `split-content`** (41 uses). These would carry the "story" sections in a defaulty preset; Pawlish doesn't tell a story page, it shows the cuts.
- **No `card-grid`, `icon-card-grid`, `bento-grid`, `numbered-cards`** as visual filler — Pawlish's services are priced lists, not feature cards.

**Catalog-rare widgets used load-bearing** (★ = ≤5 uses across existing 30 presets):
- **`comparison-slider`** ★ (5 catalog uses) — used **2x**: home opener (fullwidth) + Gallery feature transformation. The signature widget of this preset. The native trust mechanism for grooming.
- **`content-switcher`** ★ (2 catalog uses — the rarest in the catalog) — Services page pricing, toggled by dog size. A genuine UX win, not novelty.
- **`image-tabs`** ★ (4 catalog uses) — Gallery page cut-style filter (5 tabs: doodle cuts, terriers, double coats, sanitary trims, de-mat rescues). Educates customers on what a "cut style" actually is.

**Three rare widgets, plus comparison-slider load-bearing twice.** Pawlish's rare-usage signature is concentrated rather than scattered — the rare widgets do the heaviest lifting on the most important pages, in the roles they were designed for.

### Sitemap Rationale

**Six pages.** Every page earns its job:

1. **Home** — proof first, services second, voices third
2. **Services** — what we do, by size, with prices
3. **Groomers** — who handles your dog, how they handle them
4. **Gallery** — the cuts, by category, with one big transformation
5. **First Visit** — the page that defuses anxiety; explains the experience and answers the questions
6. **Contact** — book and find us

**No standalone About page.** A small grooming salon doesn't need a "founder story" page — the brand voice is established on Home, the people story lives on Groomers, and the practical "what's it like" lives on First Visit. About would be padding.

**No standalone FAQ page.** Two FAQs live where they're needed: pricing FAQ inside Services (sidebar), first-visit FAQ inside First Visit (sidebar). Questions live next to the content that triggered them.

**No standalone Pricing page.** Pricing is service-by-size and lives inside Services. Splitting them would force visitors back-and-forth.

**No blog, no portfolio-detail pages, no policies page.** Out of scope for a six-page small-business preset.

### Color Scheme Composition (cross-page)

- **`standard-primary`** is the default page surface. Most widgets sit here.
- **`standard-secondary`** is used for banding to break long runs of `standard-primary` — typically 1 use per page.
- **`highlight-primary`** is reserved for **the longevity proof** (Home key-figures) and the **Groomers credibility quote** (testimonial-hero). It signals "trust earned." Used at most once per page.
- **`highlight-secondary`** is reserved for **page-closing action-bars** — the warm espresso "we're at the end, the next step is to come in" surface. Cross-page consistency: every closing action-bar uses `highlight-secondary`.

### Spacing System

- Global `spacing_density`: `default`
- Every widget uses `top_spacing: auto` and `bottom_spacing: auto` **except**:
  - The comparison-slider opener uses `bottom_spacing: small` to tighten its handoff into the services row (the slider is visually heavy; a full gap below feels deflated).
  - No widgets use `none` — Pawlish doesn't fuse sections together. The rhythm is: each widget breathes.

### Differentiation Notes

- **Comparison-slider as the home opener.** No existing preset uses comparison-slider as a hero. For a grooming salon, the before/after IS the trust mechanism — using it on first contact is the most direct expression of the brand's value proposition. Catalog-rare widget in its native role.
- **Zero banners.** `action-bar` carries every page close, `rich-text` carries every page intro. Pawlish is the second preset (after Noteworthy) to ban the catalog's most overused widget.
- **Pricing by size, not by tier.** `content-switcher` (the rarest catalog widget) is used the way real grooming salons price — toggleable size brackets with the same service lineup per tab. This is the single most useful UX moment in the entire preset and it leans on a widget almost no other preset uses.
- **Rare-widget concentration.** Comparison-slider ×2, content-switcher ×1, image-tabs ×1 — three rare widgets, four total instances, all on pages where they're the right tool. Not novelty for novelty's sake.
- **Sitemap discipline.** Six pages, no padding. No standalone About, no standalone FAQ, no standalone Pricing — each lives where it serves the visitor. First Visit replaces the more generic "About Us" with a page that does actual work for the industry.
- **Two-FAQ strategy.** Pricing FAQ on Services, anxiety FAQ on First Visit — each FAQ lives next to the content that triggers the questions. Both use accordion sidebar layout for editorial weight. Catalog patterns usually dump every FAQ on a single page.
- **Palette:** terracotta accent on warm cream + warm espresso highlight. Distinct from every catalog preset (no terracotta-on-warm-cream pairing exists). Specifically NOT pink-spa, NOT vet-blue, NOT corporate-grey — the three default visual traps for pet sites.
- **Typography:** Fraunces 500 + Karla 400 — both first-time pairings in the catalog.
- **Closing voice.** "Bring your dog in" / "Pick a slot" / "Tell us about your dog" / "Ready when you are" — never "BOOK NOW," never urgent. Phase 0's "calm invitation" rendered through five different but consistent closings.
