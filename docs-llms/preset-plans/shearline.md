# Shearline — Preset Plan

Independent craft-forward hair salon. Small studio, 3–6 stylists, booking-driven, strong individual stylist identities. The name "Shearline" leans into the craft itself — shears, the cutting edge, a line of stylists at their chairs. Matter-of-fact, confident, slightly editorial. Not "beauty bar," not "hair spa," not "barbershop nostalgia." A place you go because the cut is precise.

## Phase 0 — Industry Strategy Brief

- **Business archetype:** Booking-based local service with strong stylist individuation. Small studio (three to six chairs), each stylist holds their own book, customers rebook with _their_ stylist every 5–10 weeks (cuts) or 8–14 weeks (color). Walk-ins are rare and usually for quick services; the core business is repeat appointments built on stylist–client relationships. Discovery is heavily Instagram-led — the website's job is to validate what the visitor already saw on a stylist's feed and move them into booking.

- **Primary conversion:** Book an appointment with a specific stylist (online via an external booking system link or a form inquiry). Secondary: color consultation request (color services usually require a pre-appointment consult — short free/paid in-person or photo-based). Tertiary: first-visit inquiry for new clients who aren't sure which stylist fits their hair.

- **Trust mechanism:** The stylist portfolio, above all. Unlike a restaurant or a dental office where the business is the brand, a hair salon is a collective of individual stylist brands sharing a roof. Trust is built by: (1) visible per-stylist work — real photos of real clients, not stock; (2) stylist specialization clarity (who does balayage, who does curls, who cuts short, who cuts men's); (3) training/credentials signals (where they trained, years in the chair, any master-stylist or color certifications); (4) the salon's shared philosophy — how consultations work, what a first visit feels like, how hair health is approached; (5) Instagram integration since feeds are where the visitor formed their opinion in the first place. Reviews matter but they confirm, they don't lead.

- **Decision mode:** Considered and emotional, with a style-research phase. Visitors often arrive with a reference (a photo, a saved post, a friend's recommendation) and want to verify the stylist can deliver it. Decisions hinge on: does this stylist's portfolio look like what I want, and can I trust them with my hair? Price sensitivity is real but secondary — people will pay more for the right cut and never go back to a cheap bad one. First-visit friction: picking _which_ stylist is the hardest step, so the site must make that choice feel guided rather than random.

- **Brand personality:** Craft-forward, editorial, confident, calmly modern. A salon that takes hair seriously as work, not as pampering. The feel of a stylist in a black apron at a clean chair with good light, a rack of shears, a wall of color swatches treated like art supplies. **NOT** a rose-gold "beauty bar" aesthetic. **NOT** a retro-barbershop aesthetic (masculine / leather / vintage tins). **NOT** a clinical trichology-clinic aesthetic. **NOT** a chain-salon corporate feel. Slightly unisex, style-aware but not fashion-obsessed, a place where a 30-year-old creative and a 55-year-old architect both feel at home.

- **Content posture:** Image-led for proof (stylist portfolios, finished looks, color work) balanced with text-led for stylist introductions and service philosophy. Pure-gallery would flatten the stylist distinctions that matter most; pure-text would waste the industry's strongest asset — the hair itself.

- **Audience model:** Three overlapping audiences the site must serve evenly, not favor one: (1) **returning clients** who know who they book with and just need the fastest path to the calendar; (2) **referred new clients** who have a stylist name or a specific look in mind and need to verify fit; (3) **new-to-the-salon clients** who aren't sure which stylist is right and need a guided path (by hair type, by service, by style). The site has to make the stylist roster legible enough that a cold visitor can pick one with confidence.

- **Required page jobs:**
  - **Home** — establish voice, lead with recent work, introduce stylists, surface booking path
  - **Services** — what's offered with price ranges, grouped by category (cut, color, treatment, extensions, bridal)
  - **Stylists** — the critical page. Per-stylist: portfolio, specialties, training, personality, direct booking link
  - **Work / Portfolio** — salon-wide gallery, browsable by category or by stylist
  - **New Clients** — what a first visit looks like, how consultations work, what to bring, hair-health approach
  - **Contact / Book** — booking paths, hours, location, how to get a color consultation

  **Six pages.** Whether Stylists and Work combine or stay separate gets resolved in Phase 1 — the argument for separation is that Work is salon-wide proof while Stylists is the roster; the argument for combination is a single visit to see both.

- **No-go patterns:**
  - Rose-gold + marble + script-font "beauty bar" aesthetic (the single most overdone hair-salon website style)
  - Scripty "luxury" fonts for headings (Parisienne, Great Vibes, Allura, etc.)
  - Stock photos of models with "perfect" hair shot against white backgrounds
  - Spa vocabulary ("pampering," "indulgence," "treat yourself," "me-time")
  - Retro-barbershop visual codes (leather, brass, chalkboard, handlebar-mustache iconography) — a different niche with its own presets
  - Pink/peach/gold palettes as the primary identity (overdone in this niche)
  - Pure all-white clinical layouts that strip warmth
  - Stacked urgent CTAs ("BOOK NOW") on every section — this is a relationship business
  - Corporate franchise-salon copy ("your destination for hair")
  - Cartoony scissor/comb/hairdryer icons used decoratively

- **Opener candidates:**
  - **Editorial-portfolio opener** — a fullwidth editorial hair photo with a short, confident one-line statement ("Cuts that hold their shape between visits.") and a quiet CTA. Leans on the strongest asset (great hair work) and sets an editorial tone on first contact.
  - **Stylist-roster opener** — introduce the people first, with a "who's at the chair" roster as the hero. Makes the stylist-individuation model structurally legible from the first scroll.
  - **Work-grid opener** — a masonry grid of recent cuts and color as the hero. Proof-first, image-dense. Risk: feels like a gallery page rather than a welcome.
  - **Split / editorial-text opener** — words on one side, a single strong image on the other. Balances craft language and visual proof without committing to either as dominant.

  No clear winner until Phase 1 — the choice hinges on whether the preset emphasizes salon voice (editorial text + image) or stylist roster (team-led). Both are defensible. **Leaning editorial-portfolio**, with the stylist roster as the second section — the editorial voice sets tone, the roster immediately delivers on the "who will cut my hair" question.

- **Closing pattern:** Calm booking invitation, stylist-linked. Language like "Pick a stylist" or "Book with the chair that fits" rather than "BOOK NOW." Because stylists hold the relationship, the close should point toward the stylist roster or directly to individual stylist booking paths, not to a generic contact form. On pages where a CTA isn't right (e.g., New Clients answering questions), a soft next-step close — "See the stylists" or "Request a color consultation" — works better than forcing a booking nudge.

## Phase 1 — Plan

### Identity

- **Name:** Shearline
- **Tagline-seed:** "Cuts that hold their shape between visits."
- **Brand world:** A craft-forward editorial hair salon. Matte-black chairs at a row of mirrors, warm wood counters, good window light, a wall of color tubes treated like art supplies, stylists in black aprons. A place that takes hair seriously as work. Gender-neutral by intent — the clientele is a 30-year-old in a linen shirt and a 55-year-old architect and a 22-year-old growing out a buzz. Editorial without being fashion-victim, confident without being cold.

### Industry Translation

- **Opener role:** Fullwidth `banner` with transparent header overlay on a strong editorial hair photograph. Heading sets the brand voice in one line ("Cuts that hold their shape between visits."); a single primary button points to the stylist roster (not to a generic contact page) — in this industry, "book" really means "pick a stylist." The page opens in the register of a magazine cover, not a service brochure.
- **Home priority order:** Editorial opener → core services in plain language → **stylist roster immediately** (the first real trust moment) → recent work → hair-philosophy editorial → parent voices → soft close. The stylists come before the work because the decision question — "who will cut my hair" — is more urgent than "can they cut hair."
- **Pricing posture:** Grouped by service category (Cuts / Color / Treatments & Add-ons), each category a tab inside `content-switcher`. Ranges, not single prices — "Women's cut — from $75" — because actual price varies with length and stylist level. Honest about consultation requirements for color work.
- **Trust posture:** Two-pronged, stylist-first. (1) Per-stylist identity: `profile-grid` treats each stylist as the star — photo, name, role, specialty, bio, Instagram link, direct booking link. This page does the heaviest lifting in the preset. (2) Salon-wide voice: editorial `image-callout` on the Home page articulates the shared philosophy (hair health, consultation approach, gender-neutral pricing, ammonia-free color options) so the individual stylists sit inside a coherent studio rather than a rented room.
- **Tone of copy:** Direct, craft-aware, unfussy. No "pamper," no "treat yourself," no "luxury," no exclamation points. Industry-fluent ("balayage," "olaplex," "single process," "gloss & tone") without being insider-obscure.

### Widget Discipline

After reading the schema for each chosen widget and considering the catalog usage rate, this plan deliberately:

**Uses as opener (intentionally, not by default):**

- **`banner`** — fullwidth, image-free in template, highlight-primary color scheme, transparent header overlay. The catalog's most overused widget gets used correctly: as the editorial magazine-cover opener the brand voice calls for. No apology.

**Catalog-rare widgets used load-bearing (★ = ≤5 uses across existing 30 presets):**

- **`content-switcher`** ★ — Services page pricing, 3 tabs (Cuts / Color / Treatments). The rarest widget in the catalog used in its native role: a toggleable pricing table. Pairs the way real salon websites present pricing.
- **`image-tabs`** ★ — Work page category browser, 5 tabs (Short cuts / Long & Lengths / Color & Balayage / Men's / Editorial & Events). Educates visitors on what the salon actually does and gives them a way to self-select into the relevant proof.

**Explicitly unsuitable for this preset:**

- **`priced-list`** — hair pricing needs toggling by category, not a flat line-item list. `content-switcher` is the right tool.
- **`pricing`** — built for SaaS tier cards, not service ranges; would force each service into a synthetic "plan" shape.
- **`split-hero`** — redundant with `banner` as opener; `banner` better supports the transparent-header editorial framing.
- **`slideshow`** — autoplaying hero slides fight the calm editorial tone.

All other catalog widgets are theme-available and not banned — just not chosen for this preset's content. A future revision could reasonably swap, say, `profile-grid` for `team-highlight` to spotlight a single stylist, or add `logo-cloud` if product-partner branding becomes part of the voice.

### Sitemap Rationale

**Six pages.** Each earns its job:

1. **Home** (`index.json`) — editorial voice, services teaser, stylist roster, recent work, philosophy, voices, close
2. **Services** (`services.json`) — what's offered with price ranges, grouped by category
3. **Stylists** (`stylists.json`) — the trust page; per-stylist portfolios, specialties, booking paths
4. **Work** (`work.json`) — salon-wide gallery, browsable by cut type
5. **New Clients** (`new-clients.json`) — first-visit walkthrough, consultation process, FAQ
6. **Contact** (`contact.json`) — book, find us, practical info

**Stylists stays separate from Work.** The argument for combining fails the audience test: returning clients head to Stylists for their person, cold visitors head to Work to browse the craft, and a merged page would make both groups scroll past the content they want. Separate pages let each task land on the first fold.

**No standalone About page.** Brand voice lives on Home's `image-callout`; the "who we are" story is carried by the stylists themselves on their dedicated page. About would be padding.

**No standalone Pricing page.** Pricing is category-by-category and lives inside Services via the content-switcher — split would force tab-back-and-forth.

**No blog, no portfolio-detail pages, no policies page.** Out of scope for a six-page small-business preset.

**Typography (first-time pairings in the catalog):**

- `heading_font`: `"EB Garamond", serif` @ `500` — a beloved editorial book-serif with real character (not another Fraunces, not another Cormorant, not another Playfair). Wide weight range (400–800). Reads as considered and craft-forward.
- `body_font`: `"Onest", sans-serif` @ `400` — a modern humanist sans, neutral enough to stay out of the heading's way, with subtle warmth. Not Inter, not DM Sans, not Karla — a conscious step off the default sans path.

**Style:**

- `corner_style`: `sharp` — matches the editorial, craft-forward register. Rounded corners would feel friendlier/softer, which is the wrong direction. Most existing presets use sharp or slightly-rounded; sharp here is a deliberate editorial choice.
- `spacing_density`: `default` — neither airy (too fashion-magazine) nor compact (too clinical).
- `button_shape`: `sharp` — aligns with the sharp corner style; reinforces editorial discipline.

### Color Scheme Composition (cross-page)

- **`standard-primary`** is the default page surface. Most widgets sit here.
- **`standard-secondary`** is used for banding to separate long runs of `standard-primary` — typically 1–2 uses per page.
- **`highlight-primary`** is reserved for **the voices** (Home testimonials) and **the stylist philosophy quote / editorial callout** moments that need weight. Used at most once per page.
- **`highlight-secondary`** is reserved for **page-closing action-bars** — the deep ink "we're at the end, the next step is to book" surface. Cross-page consistency: every closing action-bar uses `highlight-secondary`.

### Spacing System

- Global `spacing_density`: `default`
- Every widget uses `top_spacing: auto` and `bottom_spacing: auto` **except**:
  - Home `banner` opener uses `bottom_spacing: small` — the full-bleed editorial shot handing off to the services section reads better with a tightened gap than a full breath
- No widgets use `none`. Shearline does not fuse sections — the rhythm is each section breathes.

### Differentiation Notes

- **Stylist-individuation as the trust spine.** The preset runs on the assumption that a hair salon is a roof over a collection of stylist brands — `profile-grid` appears twice (Home teaser + Stylists full roster), `icon-list` doubles as a "which stylist for what" matcher, and header CTA points to `stylists.html` rather than a generic contact. Most other Arch presets center the business; this one centers the people at the chairs.
- **Content-switcher as the pricing spine.** Rarest widget in the catalog used in its native role — tab by category, range-priced. Real salons price this way; a flat priced-list would misrepresent the service.
- **Image-tabs as the portfolio browser.** Used to let cold visitors self-select into the cut category relevant to them, rather than scrolling through a salon-wide wall. Educational as well as navigational.
- **Aubergine-on-stone-with-ink.** A palette territory no existing preset occupies. Deep saturated aubergine as the signature color, warm stone (not cream) as the base, ink with faint plum undertone for highlights. Gender-neutral by design.
- **EB Garamond + Onest.** Two fonts neither of which has been paired in the catalog — editorial book-serif + modern humanist sans. Signals considered craft without defaulting to the Fraunces / Cormorant / Playfair trio the catalog already over-uses.
- **Sharp corners, sharp buttons.** Two of 28 existing presets use full-sharp. Shearline commits to sharp as an editorial decision — softness would read as hospitality, not craft.
- **Transparent-header editorial opener.** Banner + highlight-primary + transparent_on_hero gives the Home page a magazine-cover feel without relying on split-hero. First fold reads as editorial, not service-brochure.
- **Voice across closings.** "Pick a stylist" / "Know what you want?" / "Still deciding?" / "Want this for your hair?" / "Ready to book?" / "Say hello." — never "BOOK NOW," never urgent. Phase 0's "calm, stylist-linked invitation" rendered consistently across five different closings plus the Contact page acting as the close itself.
